Kepler

One astronomer who made a major advance in the understanding of our solar system and astrophysics in general was Johannes Kepler. In fact, NASA launched a satellite to search for habitable planets in 2009, and has named this mission after Kepler. In the early 1600s he published his now-famous three laws of planetary motion. These laws, based on years of stellar and planetary observation by Tycho Brahe, finally fixed any lingering anomalies in the Copernican theory that the Earth and other planets revolved around the Sun. The first law states that the planets orbited the Sun in ellipses with the Sun at one foci. The second law states that planets would travel faster the closer they are to the sun and slower when farther away.

The third law describes the relationship Kepler observed between a planet's distance from the sun and the time it takes to make one complete orbit around the sun. Kepler stated that the square of a planet's orbital period in years was equal to that planet's distance from the sun in Astronomical Units (AU) cubed, where and AU is the average distance of the Earth to the Sun (149 million kilometers).

Kepler's Third Law of Planetary Motion

\[ \huge{p^2 = a^3} \]

Newton

In 1687, Isaac Newton followed up on the laws of Kepler to publish his Principia Mathematica. In this work, he explained that it was the universal force of gravity which tied together the motion of the planets and the motion of objects here on Earth. Kepler's third law was found to be a special case of a more general law about the gravitational attraction between two objects in space, \(M_1\) and \(M_2\). Now, instead of Kepler's law being tied to the Earth and the Sun, we can now calculate the orbital period of any planet around any star as long as we know both their masses and the average distance of the star from the planet.

Newton's Revised Law of Planetary Motion

\[ \huge{p^2 = \frac{4 \pi^2}{G(M_1 + M_2)} a^3} \]

Again, \(p\) is the period of the planet, measured in seconds. \(M_1\) and \(M_2\) are the masses of the planet and its star in kilograms. \(G\) is the Gravitational Constant, which is approximately \( G = 6.67 \times 10^{-11} \) (measured in units of \(\mathrm{m}^3/(\mathrm{kg} \, \mathrm{s}^2)\)). Remember that you can enter numbers in scientific notation using Python syntax like 6.67e-11. To access \(\pi\), you can add the line 

import math
to the top of your program, and then use math.pi anywhere in your program that you need the value of \(\pi\).

Just like before, your program will need to find \(a\), the distance between the planet and star. You will need to solve the equation for \(a\) to figure out what Python code to write. It's OK if you don't remember how to do this—just ask for help!

Step 2

Make a copy of orbit_kepler.py called orbit_newton.py. Now modify orbit_newton.py to use Newton's revised law of planetary motion instead of Kepler's law.
  • The user should be prompted to enter
    • the name of the planet,
    • the orbital period of the planet in days,
    • the mass of the star in kilograms,
    • and the mass of the planet in kilograms.
  • Newton's formula requires the period in seconds, so you will need to convert the user's input from days into seconds.
  • Calculate the distance of the star from the planet using Newton's formula.
  • Since Newton's law uses meters instead of AU, you will have to convert the output from meters to AU, using the fact that 1 AU is equal to 149 million kilometers.
